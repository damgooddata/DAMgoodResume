# Cover Letter Anti-Patterns — Do Not Repeat

This file documents specific mistakes to avoid when writing cover letters and proposals for David Morgan / DAMgoodData. These were identified from real feedback and should be treated as hard rules.

---

## Voice & Tone Failures

### Do not write like a document. Write like David talks.
David's voice is conversational, direct, and confident. It flows like a letter from one professional to another. It does not read like a structured report with labeled sections.

### Do not use bold headers as section dividers.
Bad: `**The Architecture — How These Agents Actually Work:**`
Bad: `**Where I've done this before:**`
Bad: `**What I'd bring to your engagement:**`

Good: Bold is used sparingly for emphasis on specific terms or concepts inline, not as document structure. The letter should flow without needing headers to organize it.

### Do not explain concepts like a tutorial or whitepaper.
Bad: "MCP Servers act as standardized connectors between your agents and external services... Instead of hardcoding every integration, MCP gives each agent a universal adapter..."
Bad: "Hooks provide safety and governance at every decision point..."

Good: Drop technical terms naturally, assume the reader is smart enough to follow. "I connect systems using MCP rather than brittle custom wrappers" — done, move on.

### Do not use numbered agent labels.
Bad: "Agent 1 — Life Todos", "Agent 2 — Bill Pay", "Agent 3 — Property Manager"

Good: Describe each system with a natural descriptor. "Life Todos & Project Tracker", "Bill Pay Tracker", "Smart Property Manager". Lead with what it does, not a number.

---

## Structure Failures

### Do not over-structure with bullet-heavy architecture sections.
David's letters use bullet points selectively — for a phased approach list or a short feature set. They do not use bullets to explain every architectural component in a labeled taxonomy.

### Do not front-load a generic fit statement.
Bad: "This is right in my wheelhouse."

Good: Open with something specific to the client's problem or a direct statement of capability. The fit should be obvious from the content, not declared upfront.

### Do not separate "proof" from "approach" into distinct labeled sections.
David weaves proof points into the approach naturally. "I built X at Y, which is the same pattern I would use here" — not a proof section followed by an approach section.

### Do not add a signature block with pipe separators.
Bad: "— David Morgan | DAMgoodData"

The closing should feel natural and conversational, not formatted like an email signature.

---

## Content Failures

### Do not over-explain how the technology works.
The client does not need a paragraph explaining what structured outputs are or how hooks validate actions. They need to know that David builds systems with built-in safety and that agents talk to each other cleanly. One sentence, not a paragraph.

### Do not write a RAG explainer.
Bad: "it runs a sentiment-aware interrogation loop: it queries your vector-stored documentation (lease terms, payment history, prior communications, SOPs), evaluates the tone and intent of the request, and follows up with clarifying questions if confidence is low."

Good: "If an input is ambiguous, the AI asks you clarifying questions rather than guessing." The value is the behavior, not the mechanism.

### Do not pad with filler phrases.
Bad: "This isn't optional — it's how you keep agents from doing something you didn't authorize."
Bad: "Here's what that means in practice:"

These add length without adding value. Cut them.

### Do not use corporate hedging language.
Bad: "I'd bring to your engagement"
Bad: "What I'd bring to this"

Good: "I can build this." "Here is how I would approach it." Direct, no hedging.

---

## Formatting Rules

- Use line breaks between thoughts for readability, not dense paragraphs
- Bold sparingly — key product names, dollar figures, or a concept that needs emphasis
- Bullets only for phased approaches or short feature lists, not for explaining architecture
- Keep paragraphs to 2-4 sentences max
- Technical terms are dropped in naturally, never defined or explained unless the explanation IS the point
- The letter should be readable in under 2 minutes

---

## The Standard to Hit

Every cover letter should read like David wrote it in 15 minutes because he's done this work before and knows exactly how to talk about it. Confident, specific, practical, conversational. No fluff, no structure-for-structure's-sake, no tutorial tone.
