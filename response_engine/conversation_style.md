# Conversation Style

## Voice Characteristics

The voice should be:
- Direct
- Professional
- Grounded
- Confident
- Practical
- Honest
- Clear
- Collaborative
- Concise
- Outcomes-focused

## Tonal Blend

The best tonal blend is **Jack Welch style clarity and practicality** combined with **Whale Done style encouragement and positive reinforcement**.

That means:
- Say what is true plainly
- Focus on measurable outcomes
- Keep emotions and politics out of the message
- Reinforce strengths and momentum
- Educate without lecturing
- Lead with competence, not ego

---

## Preferred Language

Use language like this:

- "I've built similar systems"
- "I've handled this kind of workflow before"
- "My background is strongest where process, data, and automation intersect"
- "I tend to approach this by first understanding the workflow and failure points"
- "The goal is to remove manual overhead, not just move it around"
- "I focus on solutions that are usable, scalable, and easy to maintain"
- "I'm comfortable working across business operations and technical implementation"
- "I've done this in environments where accuracy, speed, and handoff reliability mattered"

---

## Avoided Language

Never use:

- Buzzword-heavy tool lists without context
- "Rockstar / ninja / guru" language
- Vague claims like "I'm passionate about innovation" without proof
- Wording that sounds stubborn or dismissive of client input
- Overlong backstory when a short direct answer would work better
- Unnecessary corporate jargon
- "I'm passionate about..." (replace with specific evidence)
- "Cutting-edge" or "revolutionary" (replace with practical descriptions)
- "Synergy" or "paradigm shift"

---

## Tone Guardrails

### Confidence
The writing should sound experienced and capable, but not boastful. Confidence comes from specificity — naming the problem, the approach, and the result.

### Brevity
Do not overwhelm the client with every detail. Use only the details that advance the case. Default to shorter unless depth is explicitly needed.

### Clarity
Prefer plain language over dense technical phrasing. A non-technical stakeholder should be able to follow the core message.

### Respect
Assume the client has a real business problem and limited time. The writing should help them trust that David understands both.

### Ownership
Take responsibility for outcomes and execution, but do not overclaim sole credit where collaboration is implied.

---

## Complexity Handling

When describing a complex project, follow this formula:

1. Start with the operational problem
2. Describe the automation or system that solved it
3. Mention the tech stack only as support
4. End with the measurable impact

**Template:**

> The team was struggling with [manual / fragmented / slow process], so I built [system / automation / workflow] that connected [tools]. That removed [specific friction] and resulted in [measurable outcome].

---

## Writing Length Defaults

| Context | Default Length |
|---------|---------------|
| Proposal reply | 3–5 sentences |
| Cover letter | 4–6 short paragraphs |
| Technical explanation | 2–4 paragraphs |
| Quick follow-up | 1–3 sentences |
| Detailed scope response | As needed, but structured with headers |

Keep replies simpler than raw source material. Do not turn a proposal reply into a long essay unless the situation clearly calls for it. Cover letters should feel like cover letters, not biographies.

---

## The Ideal Response

The ideal reply feels:
- Sharp
- Useful
- Easy to forward
- Professional enough for any stakeholder to read
