# Client Analysis Framework

When analyzing a job post, client request, or inbound inquiry, do not stop at the listed tasks. Use this 5-layer framework to unpack the hidden narrative and build a response from deeper understanding.

---

## Layer 1: Stated Need

What does the client say they want?

Ask yourself:
- What specific task or deliverable did they name?
- What tools or platforms did they mention?
- What role or title are they hiring for?

Examples:
- Salesforce migration
- Access database help
- Reporting automation
- API integration
- Web app rebuild
- Dashboard creation
- Process documentation

---

## Layer 2: Operational Pain

What is probably actually going wrong?

Look for signals of:
- Manual steps consuming staff time
- Users who do not trust the data
- Process that breaks at handoffs between people or systems
- Current tools that do not scale
- Work that depends on one person (bus factor = 1)
- Systems that are not talking to each other
- Repeated rework or error correction
- Workarounds that have become permanent

---

## Layer 3: Business Risk

What is the cost of not fixing it?

Consider:
- Missed revenue or delayed billing
- Delayed decision-making due to bad or slow data
- Compliance or audit risk
- Payment delays (vendor or customer)
- Operational drag that limits growth
- Poor user adoption of current tools
- Inability to scale without adding headcount
- Single points of failure in critical processes

---

## Layer 4: Emotional Narrative

What does the buyer likely need to feel?

The client is often looking for someone who makes them feel:
- "This person understands the mess I'm dealing with"
- "This person can handle both technical and practical realities"
- "This person won't create a bigger mess"
- "This person can make this simpler for my team"
- "This person has done this before — I'm not their experiment"
- "This person will actually finish and hand off something usable"

Responses should address at least one of these emotional needs implicitly.

---

## Layer 5: Strategic Match

How does David uniquely fit this need?

Connect the problem to:
- Enterprise-scale automation experience
- Microsoft ecosystem depth (Excel, Access, Power BI, Power Automate, SQL Server)
- Python/API/SQL integration skill
- Salesforce integration ability (Apex, Flows, LWC, API)
- Experience with messy real-world operations (not just clean-room environments)
- Ability to design tools non-technical teams can actually use
- History of building durable systems with measurable impact
- Cross-industry adaptability (retail, supply chain, logistics, finance, nonprofit, education, legal)

---

## How to Use This Framework

### For a proposal reply:
1. Identify Layers 1-2 from the client's post
2. Build your response from Layer 4 (emotional reassurance) and Layer 5 (strategic match)
3. Include one proof point from `proof_points.md` that maps to Layer 2 or Layer 3

### For a cover letter:
1. Work through all 5 layers
2. Lead with Layer 1 acknowledgment
3. Body should address Layers 2-3 implicitly
4. Close with Layer 5 positioning

### For a follow-up message:
1. Reference Layer 1 (what they asked about)
2. Add value from Layer 2 (show you understand deeper)
3. Keep it short — 2-3 sentences

### For a technical explanation:
1. Start with Layer 2 (the real operational problem)
2. Walk through the solution approach
3. End with Layer 3 reframing (what this prevents or enables)

---

## Red Flags to Watch For

When analyzing client requests, note these signals that may require David's direct input:

- Scope that sounds like multiple full-time roles combined
- Budget language that suggests they want enterprise work at freelance prices
- Requests that name very specific tools but describe problems those tools don't solve
- Posts that list 15+ technologies as "required" — likely unclear on what they actually need
- Language suggesting they've burned through previous contractors
